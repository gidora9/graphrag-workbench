1. Executive Summary
The global mobility market is undergoing a profound transformation, driven by the 
normalization of remote work and the rise of the digital nomad. This report provides a 
comprehensive analysis of this evolving landscape, with a particular focus on the Iberian 
Peninsula as a key hub. Our analysis, based on the provided data and enriched with 
external research, identifies two primary market segments: Digital Nomad (DN) Seekers, 
who prioritize transactional, short-term residency, and Passport Seekers, who pursue 
strategic, long-term citizenship pathways.
The market is projected to experience significant growth, with the corporate relocation 
services sector expected to grow from $10.93 billion in 2025 to $21.21 billion by 2033 1. 
This expansion is mirrored in the rapid growth of the digital nomad population, which is 
estimated to reach 40 million globally by 2025 2. Spain has emerged as the number one 
destination for this demographic, leveraging its progressive Startup Law and favorable 
Beckham Law tax regime to attract high-value talent 2.
This report examines the competitive landscape, segmenting players into four distinct 
categories: Traditional Relocation Management Companies (RMCs), Tech-Enabled 
Platforms, Specialized Legal/Tax Firms, and Niche Local Providers. We conclude with 
strategic recommendations, highlighting the opportunity to capture the underserved mid-
market enterprise segment in Spain by offering an integrated, tech-enabled, compliance-
first service model.

3. Deep Dive: The Digital Nomad Phenomenon
The most significant catalyst in the modern mobility landscape is the rise of the digital 
nomad. This demographic, once a fringe subculture, has expanded into a formidable 
economic force, compelling governments and corporations to adapt.
•
Population Growth: The global digital nomad population is estimated to be around 40 
million individuals in 2025, a doubling from just a few years prior. This cohort is young, 
affluent, and highly skilled, with 79% earning over $50,000 annually and an average 
salary of $124,416 2.
•
Economic Impact: Digital nomads are not just travelers; they are "mobile micro-
economies." Their presence stimulates local economies through spending on 
accommodation, coworking spaces, and services. The coworking sector alone was 
valued at nearly $15 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach up to $46 billion by 2030 
2.
•
Strategic Importance: In a climate of declining Foreign Direct Investment (FDI), which 
fell by 11% in 2024, digital nomads represent an FDI-adjacent channel of human and 
financial capital. Governments are increasingly using Digital Nomad Visas (DNVs) not 
just to attract talent but to seed startups and diversify investment 2.

As of 2025, 64 countries offer DNVs, with over 90% of these programs created after 2020. 
Europe leads in offering structured, long-term integration pathways, while Latin America 
provides accelerated citizenship routes, and the UAE excels with a tax-free, premium 
lifestyle offering 2.

The Digital Nomad Visa (DNV) serves as a critical bridge between these two segments. It 
provides an immediate residency solution for DN Seekers while simultaneously offering 
Passport Seekers a legitimate, multi-year pathway toward permanent residency and 
citizenship. The key distinction lies in the service complexity: DN Seekers require efficient, 
transactional visa success, whereas Passport Seekers need sophisticated, long-term legal 
and tax advisory to ensure compliance over the 2- to 10-year journey to naturalization.
5. Competitive Landscape: Mobility Services in Iberia
The mobility services market in Spain and Portugal is a complex ecosystem of players with 
varying service scopes and delivery models. The competitive landscape can be visualized 
across two primary axes: Service Scope & Compliance Complexity (from transactional 
visa processing to strategic, long-term advisory) and Digital Agility & Transparent Pricing 
(from traditional, opaque models to modern, tech-driven platforms).

This landscape is segmented into four main quadrants:
1.Quadrant 1: Full-Service Compliance Guardians: These are the traditional, high-touch 
Relocation Management Companies (RMCs) like Santa Fe Relocation and Eres 
Relocation Services. They serve large enterprise clients, offering comprehensive, end-
to-end management but are often characterized by higher costs and less transparent 
pricing.
2.Quadrant 2: Integrated Tech/Compliance Hybrids: This is the emerging sweet spot. 
Players like Lexidy and Carbray International blend deep legal and tax expertise with 
modern, tech-enabled service delivery. They target the mid-market with fixed-fee 
proposals and a focus on high-value compliance, such as navigating Spain’s Beckham 
Law. Their success is evidenced by offerings like Lexidy’s 100% refund guarantee on 
visa rejections.
3.Quadrant 3: Global Digital Processors: These are pure-tech, high-volume platforms 
like Deel, Jobbatical, and Remote.com. They leverage their Employer of Record (EOR) 
status and automated platforms to offer scalable, efficient immigration and payroll 
solutions, primarily targeting tech-forward companies and remote-first teams.
4.Quadrant 4: Local Niche Specialists: This quadrant includes highly focused providers 
that excel in a specific area. Examples include Taxpats and NIM Lawyers (specializing 

in tax optimization) and DigitalNomadVisaInSpain (offering a unique contingent-fee 
model for DNV applications). They compete on deep expertise and client-friendly 
financial models rather than scale.
6. Niche Market Analysis: Spain as a Premier Hub
Spain has strategically positioned itself to become the #1 global destination for digital 
nomads in 2025 2. This success is not accidental but the result of a concerted policy effort 
to create a highly attractive ecosystem for mobile talent.
•
Progressive Legal Framework: The Startup Law (2022) created a formal international 
teleworking visa and, crucially, allows nomads to earn up to 20% of their income from 
local Spanish clients, facilitating deeper economic integration.
•
Favorable Tax Regime: The Beckham Law (Special Tax Regime) offers a significant 
financial incentive, allowing eligible newcomers to be taxed at a flat rate of 24% on 
Spanish-sourced income up to €600,000 for six years. This provides predictability and 
substantial savings.
•
Pathway to EU Citizenship: Spain offers one of the few direct pathways from a DNV to 
citizenship. This is particularly advantageous for nationals of Ibero-American countries, 
who can apply for citizenship after just two years of legal residence.
•
Superior Infrastructure: Spain boasts world-class digital connectivity, with fixed 
broadband speeds that are consistently among the fastest in Europe, alongside a high 
quality of life and a lower cost of living than many Northern European counterparts 2.
This combination of legal, financial, and lifestyle advantages creates a powerful value 
proposition, positioning Spain not just as a temporary destination but as a strategic hub for 
long-term settlement and integration into the European Union.
7. Strategic Recommendations
Based on the market analysis, a significant opportunity exists to capture the underserved 
mid-market enterprise (MME) and tech scale-up segment in Spain. These companies 
require reliable, predictable compliance and relocation support but often find traditional 
RMCs too costly and pure-tech platforms lacking in specialized advisory.
1.Adopt a Hybrid, Tech-Enabled Model: Develop a service offering that combines the 
legal and tax depth of a Tier 1 firm with the efficiency and transparency of a tech 
platform. This aligns with the successful Quadrant 2 positioning.
2.Focus on Integrated Risk Management: Bundle DNV filing with high-value advisory 
services, such as corporate Permanent Establishment (PE) risk audits and 

STR/Beckham Law consultation. This elevates the service from a transactional 
commodity to a strategic partnership.
3.Implement a Fixed-Price, Transparent Model: Adopt the fixed-fee proposal model 
proven successful by players like Carbray and Lexidy. This directly addresses the mid-
market’s demand for budget predictability and counters the opacity of traditional 
RMCs.
4.Target the €8,000‒€12,000 Price Point: Position the service within this competitive yet 
underserved pricing band to attract MMEs that are priced out by large RMCs but require 
more than a basic visa filing service.
5.Leverage B2B Partnerships: Instead of competing directly with global EOR/HR 
platforms (Quadrant 3), act as their local service partner in Iberia. This B2B model 
leverages their vast customer acquisition channels while providing the critical, deep 
local expertise they lack.
8. Conclusion
The global mobility landscape is being reshaped by the convergence of technology, flexible 
work policies, and strategic government action. The digital nomad is no longer a transient 
tourist but a key economic actor, sought after by nations for their skills, capital, and 
entrepreneurial potential. Spain has masterfully capitalized on this trend, creating a best-in-
class ecosystem that balances lifestyle appeal with substantive legal and financial 
incentives.
For service providers, the path forward lies not in competing on scale alone, but in 
delivering integrated, high-value solutions. By focusing on the compliance-heavy, 
strategically minded needs of the mid-market and leveraging a tech-enabled, transparent 
service model, a provider can establish a defensible and highly profitable niche in the 
world’s most dynamic mobility market.
